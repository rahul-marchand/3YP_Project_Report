\chapter{Hardware}
\label{chapter:headset}

The system is conceived as a low-cost, head-mounted device that standardises the geometry between a user's eyes, a stimulus display, and an imaging sensor in order to perform multi-parameter oculomotor assessment using pupillometry. The headset provides:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A controlled optical pathway from a display to the user's eyes, allowing presentation of pupillary light reflex, smooth pursuit, and vergence stimuli.

    \item A stable, near-frontal view of both eyes for image-based tracking of pupil size and gaze.

    \item A mechanically constrained, repeatable eye--camera distance across users.
\end{itemize}

To remain compatible with deployment in grassroots sports, the design prioritises minimal custom electronics and leverages consumer hardware wherever possible. The headset therefore acts primarily as an optical and mechanical fixture around a smartphone with only simple ancillary electronics (e.g. LEDs) where required.

A key architectural decision is the use of a partially reflective beamsplitter positioned at 45° between the eyes and a display located above the field of view. This beamsplitter performs two functions simultaneously: it reflects the display image into the eyes, mimicking a compact VR headset, and it transmits light from the eyes to a camera located behind the beamsplitter on the same optical axis as the lenses. By keeping the camera bay dark and the display bright, the user perceives only the reflected display image, whilst the camera looks through the beamsplitter at the illuminated eyes. This arrangement resolves the conflict between the need for a straight-on eye view and the requirement that the camera not intrude into the visual field.


\section{Design Criteria}

The hardware must satisfy a set of quantitative and qualitative requirements derived from the clinical use case, the chosen biomarkers, and the intended deployment environment. For clarity, these are grouped into optical, geometric, user-facing, and practical constraints.

\subsection{Optical and Imaging Requirements}

\textbf{Pupil visibility and resolution.} The camera must resolve the pupillary margin sufficiently for accurate segmentation and diameter measurement on each frame. This implies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Eye region spanning a substantial fraction of the frame (e.g. > 200--300 pixels across the iris for typical smartphone resolutions).

    \item Sufficient contrast between iris and pupil under controlled illumination.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Viewing angle and distortion.} The camera should view each eye approximately along its optical axis, minimising foreshortening and perspective distortion that would complicate gaze estimation.

\textbf{Controlled illumination.} The optical system must:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Provide enough light to achieve low-noise images at video frame rates.

    \item Avoid large specular glares that obscure the pupil.

    \item Support controlled light stimuli for PLR (rapid change in illumination) without saturating the sensor or causing discomfort.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Stimulus--eye relationship.} The geometry between the eye and the display must be known and stable so that the position of a dot or other stimuli on the screen can be related to an expected gaze direction.

\subsection{Geometric and Mechanical Requirements}

\textbf{Fixed eye--camera distance.} The headset must constrain the distance and relative orientation between eyes and camera to within a tight tolerance across users (e.g. variations on the order of a few millimetres), ensuring that calibration performed once is valid for repeated tests.

\textbf{Accommodation comfort.} The virtual distance of the viewed image must fall within a comfortable accommodative range for children and adolescents, and must not induce significant eye strain during testing.

\textbf{Alignment repeatability.} The design should assist users in positioning the headset correctly (e.g. via nose bridge, straps, and padding) so that the eye-box (region where both eyes see the full display and are fully visible to the camera) is easy to locate.

\subsection{User-Facing Requirements}

\textbf{Comfort and safety.} The headset must be lightweight, distribute pressure evenly, avoid sharp edges, and be tolerable for a few minutes of use immediately after a suspected head injury.

\textbf{Low cognitive and visual load.} Optical design should avoid unusual or distracting artefacts (e.g. a visible camera in one eye) that could be unsettling, especially for symptomatic users.

\subsection{Practical and Economic Requirements}

\textbf{Low cost.} The bill of materials (excluding the user's smartphone) should fall in the £30--£80 range, precluding the use of multiple high-speed IR cameras or custom microdisplays.

\textbf{Robustness and manufacturability.} Components should be readily available, mechanically robust, and tolerant of repeated handling in school and club environments.

\textbf{Modularity.} The design should permit substitution of different smartphones (within a size range) and potential future upgrades (e.g. adding IR LEDs) without complete redesign.

These requirements guided the exploration of candidate optical and mechanical configurations.


\section{Optics}

\subsection{Direct Viewing Without Lenses}

A natural baseline configuration places the display at a distance where users can focus without additional optics, and positions the camera near the display to observe the eyes. A prototype implementing this concept used a smartphone screen at distances in the range 20--30~cm from the eyes, mounted within an extended headset ``tube''.

Informal testing with multiple users showed that whilst some individuals could accommodate at these distances, others struggled to achieve a clear image or reported rapid onset of eye strain. Reported comfortable focus distances varied substantially between users, and increasing the display distance to accommodate those with poorer near vision produced an impractically large form factor.

This approach failed several requirements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accommodation comfort and variability.} The design relied on the user's accommodative ability at relatively close distances, producing an unacceptable spread in user experience and risking exacerbation of symptoms in recently concussed individuals.

    \item \textbf{Form factor.} Achieving a universally comfortable focus distance without optics implied a very deep headset, undermining portability and comfort.
\end{itemize}

These results motivated the introduction of lenses to decouple the physical display distance from the perceived focus distance.

\section{Electronics}
\label{sec:electronics}


\subsection{System Requirements and Objectives}
\label{sec:electronics-requirements}

\subsubsection{Purpose of the Electronics Subsystems}

The purpose of the electronic subsystem within the headset is to enable precise, repeatable and time-stamped pupillometry data collection and inference, since quantitative pupil and eye-movement metrics have been reported as relevant markers of neurological dysfunction~\cite{mcdonald2022eye}\todo{Verify citation: original had [1] --- replaced with McDonald et al.\ 2022 (eye movement biomarkers review)}. In order to meet its objectives, the subsystem must include devices which are able to (i)~present visual stimuli, (ii)~provide stable near-eye IR illumination, (iii)~capture synchronised binocular eye video, (iv)~timestamp and store recorded data points and (v)~process and run inference on recorded video.

\subsubsection{Measurement Requirements}

Having covered the purpose of the electronics subsystems, the next step is to highlight the overarching specification requirements based on the biological features of the chosen biomarkers.

In order to capture pupillary constriction dynamics, vergence response latency, and smooth pursuit tracking behaviour, which occur at short time intervals, the electronics system must support high frame-rate eye measurement of ${\sim}120$\,Hz~\cite{bergamin2003pupil}\todo{Verify citation: original had [2] --- replaced with Bergamin \& Kardon 2003 (PLR sample rate study)}.

Identifying the vergence biomarker also requires an analysis of the coordinated behaviour of both eyes~\cite{yaramothu2019vergence}\todo{Verify citation: original had [3] --- replaced with Yaramothu et al.\ 2019 (vergence endurance as concussion biomarker)}. Therefore, the design must include binocular capture with balanced near-eye IR illumination.

\subsubsection{Use-Case Driven System Requirements}

The electronics architecture must reflect the device's use case, which is that of a portable screening tool for pitch-side use in amateur sport. Therefore, the prototype must be (i)~battery-powered, (ii)~self-contained and (iii)~wearable, (iv)~intuitive enough to be operated by a user without a medical/technology background (v)~basic resistance to sweat and rain.

This introduces the following practical and safety system requirements. The system must be capable of (i)~operating locally without continuous internet connectivity, (ii)~managing connectivity interruptions, (iii)~interrupting sessions in the case of head-movement (iv)~activating guard-rail procedures to protect user in the event of illumination, battery, and overheating mishaps.

From a design criteria perspective, the project requires the device to adhere to (i)~accessibility (ii)~accuracy and (iii)~convenience.

The Compute, Storage and Communications Subsystem is the central coordination block of the device. It consists of an embedded computer, local session storage and Integrated Circuits required for wireless communication. The responsibilities of the subsystem include issuing control actions to the sensing and stimulus hardware, receiving test data streams, storing captured data locally, carrying out post-capture inference and handling wireless communications with the external Mobile App + Backend Cloud Infrastructure.

The Sensing Subsystem consists of electronics that record measurement data from the user, which is then sent to the central computational block. These include a binocular camera pair and an IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit). The camera provides eye-imaging capabilities used for the extraction of pupil and eye-movement features and the IMU provides head motion related information used for a session validity assessment.

The Stimulus and Illumination Subsystem contains the hardware responsible for producing adequate optical conditions for \ab{plr}, \ab{npc} and smooth pursuit tests as outlined in \Cref{sec:bg-oculomotor}. These include an internal display to present the visual stimulus to the subject as well as near-eye IR illumination to ensure reliable eye imaging. Together, these form an ideal measurement environment.

The Power and Protection Subsystem is not only responsible for supply distribution but also for housing vital monitoring and protection systems. It contains the battery source, charging paths, monitoring electronics, and protective mechanisms associated with illumination control, low-battery handling, electrical safety and fault response.

\subsubsection{Operating Modes and Data Flow}

The headset supports two operating modes: a data collection mode (non-concussed user) and a pitch side inference mode (potentially concussed user). The difference in these operating modes lies primarily in the timing, volume and method of external data transfer. The software aspect associated with these operating modes is highlighted in \Cref{chapter:software}.

Both modes start with the compute, storage and communications subsystem coordinating stimulus presentation, sensing activity and event logging during the capture window. The binocular eye video and IMU data are captured locally and written to on-device storage alongside any associated session metadata.

In the data collection mode, the complete session data is retained for future off-device use. This includes the binocular video, IMU data, event logs and timestamps and extracted features. Instead of transferring the data alongside inference, the headset stores the session data locally and then uploads it to backend cloud storage over Wi-Fi at a later time. Wi-Fi was chosen for this model due to heavy bandwidth requirement for transmission and the flexibility of being able to achieve a deferred upload once a strong Wi-Fi connection is achieved.

In the pitch side inference mode, the headset performs local inference from the captured session data. The headset then sends a compact results packet to the mobile app over Bluetooth. Bluetooth was chosen as the transmission medium for this mode due to the small data packet being transferred and the mobile app is required to be in close proximity to headset anyway. If the connection is interrupted, the headset retains the inference results locally and transmits it when the link is restored.


\subsection{Camera and I/O Systems}

\subsubsection{Role and Design of the Camera Subsystem}

\Cref{sec:electronics-requirements} highlighted that the headset must be able to support binocular eye imaging at 120\,Hz. The role of this section is to resolve the following practical questions associated with the camera subsystem and outline the well-reasoned camera module choice.

At a subsystem level, there are 4 main design choices that will be addressed in the following subsections:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Whether the operating point should be VGA or 720p?
    \item Whether to opt for fixed focus or auto-focus?
    \item Whether to incorporate rolling shutter or global shutter?
    \item Whether to use an embedded or general purpose camera?
\end{enumerate}

Answering these questions will further help determine the bandwidth, storage and packaging requirements for the headset.

\subsubsection{Camera Subsystem Architecture}

This subsection justifies the camera-subsystem design choices introduced above.

The imaging task is not a full-face capture but reliable localisation of the pupil boundary and eye position under near-eye geometry. The required frame rate has already been established as 120\,Hz. More broadly, eye-tracking literature has justified a lower spatial resolution as a baseline when temporal fidelity is prioritised in near-eye eye tracking, as it enables higher frame rates with sufficient eye-region detail~\cite{morimoto2005eye, chugh2020eye}\todo{Verify citations: original had [Chugh 2020, other one(2004)] --- replaced with Morimoto \& Mimica 2005 + Chugh 2020}. Therefore, to minimise load on the processing unit, a VGA operating point can be justified over a 720p resolution.

A fixed-focus optical solution is also preferred. The headset geometry fixes the camera-to-eye spacing, with minimal variation from user-to-user. Under these conditions, auto-focus would introduce additional control complexity, power burden and software overhead with limited practical benefits. Fixed-focus optics therefore align best with the previously outlined design criteria of ease of use, accessible and robust.

The next choice concerns shutter behaviour within the camera. In a global-shutter sensor, all pixels are exposed to light simultaneously, whereas in a rolling-shutter sensor, the image is read line by line over a short time interval (\Cref{fig:shutter-comparison}). When trying to capture eye motion using a rolling-shutter sensor, intra-frame temporal skews and geometric distortion become present~\cite{farivar2016eyetracker}\todo{Verify citation: original had \{ref2 (paper)\} --- replaced with Farivar \& Michaud-Landry 2016 (global shutter requirement for eye tracking)}. Considering this, a global-shutter sensor is an appropriate choice.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/electronics_image2}
    \caption{Comparison of global shutter and rolling shutter capture of a rotating fan, illustrating the geometric distortion introduced by rolling shutter readout.}
    \label{fig:shutter-comparison}
\end{figure}

The final design choice is that of the camera interface. The choice lies between an embedded camera module, a compact PCB-level imaging module, and a general purpose camera, an externally packaged device connected to the headset via USB. For a self-contained headset, the embedded approach is preferred due to its low integration risk, manageable wiring and suitable data path.

\subsection{Illumination and Driver Electronics}

\subsubsection{Role of the Illumination Subsystem}

The illumination subsystem within the headset must be designed to satisfy 3 principal design requirements. First, it must provide sufficient iris-to-pupil contrast to support image segmentation. Second, it must minimise sensitivity to facial shadowing and headset fitting variance, allowing for repeatable measurements across sessions. Finally, it must provide uniform and symmetric illumination across both eyes, so binocular differences arise mainly from pupillary differences rather than lighting conditions.

\subsubsection{Wavelength Selection and Optical Arrangement}

The near-eye illumination must be selected such that the lighting does not interfere with the eye tracking process when the visual stimulus is presented on the internal display. To achieve this, near-infrared illumination must be used. It has been widely accepted in camera-based eye tracking as it improves pupil visibility at no cost of stimulus hindrance~\cite{hansen2010eye}\todo{Verify citation: original had [kim et al.] --- replaced with Hansen \& Ji 2010 (definitive eye/gaze survey; ``kim et al.'' not identifiable)}. An appropriate wavelength of light must be chosen with regard to sensor sensitivity, cost and cornea exposure safety. 850\,nm was chosen as appropriate based on its prior use in eye-imaging as well as it being invisible to the eye, therefore suppressing the dazzling light effect.

The physical arrangement adopted was that of an LED illumination ring around each camera. This geometry allows for symmetric distribution of light across the eye, reduces local shadowing and helps maintain a consistent pupil appearance across the tests.

\subsubsection{Driver Topology and Electrical Implementation}

This subsection justifies the operational and driver choices associated with the illumination subsystem. The main electrical criterion is the need for a stable illumination output whilst minimising power loss and heat generation. Since LED optical output is primarily determined by drive current, and a small change in forward voltage can result in a large change in forward current, a constant-current driver is preferred~\cite{ti2007an1656}\todo{Verify citation: original had [bridgelux] --- replaced with TI AN-1656 (LED driver design challenges)}.

A specific current can be delivered to the LED via a linear current regulator or a switched buck current driver. While a linear current regulator is a simple solution, any excess voltage between the battery rail and the LED would be dissipated as heat. This is undesirable within the headset so a buck regulator becomes the more efficient choice~\cite{ti2007an1656}\todo{Verify citation: original had [Texas instruments] --- same TI AN-1656 covers both constant-current and buck topology}.

Finally, the 2 LED rings will comprise of parallel components as opposed to a single series chain. This means that a single branch failure will not disable a full ring thus allowing for the device to continue operating. This improves robustness and practical integration of the headset.

The device adopts a fixed brightness level during capture which is established by a one-time calibration prior to the data collection. \todo{Complete: original text appears truncated here.}

\subsubsection{Safety and Fail-Safe Behaviour}

As the illumination subsystem operates close to the eye, safety frameworks such as IEC~62471 must be incorporated~\cite{iec62471}\todo{Verify citation: original referenced IEC 62471 inline --- added formal standard reference}. This evaluates the photobiological safety of light systems using exposure limits depending on intensity, viewing distance and exposure duration. Specifically, the corneal irradiance and retinal thermal hazard tests must be carried out.

For the proposed headset, the maximum continued IR exposure duration is ${\sim}30$\,s, where the lights are positioned ${\sim}7$\,cm from the eye. The corneal exposure test requires us to follow the sub-1000\,s IEC relation~\cite{osram2017iec62471}\todo{Verify citation: original had [Luxecon] --- likely meant Luxeon/Lumileds; replaced with OSRAM AN050 (IEC 62471 application guide)}, which states the upper bound on W/sr that the LED can safely emit.

We first find the exposure limit of the eye by:
%
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{IR,lim}} = 18000 \, t^{-3/4} = 18000 \times 30^{-3/4} \approx 1404 \; \text{W/m}^{2}
\end{equation}

From which we can find the upper bound on the power that the source sends per unit solid angle by:
%
\begin{equation}
    I_{\max} = E_{\text{IR,lim}} \times d^{2} \approx 1404 \times 0.07^{2} \approx 6.88 \; \text{W/sr}
\end{equation}

This shows that the total radiant intensity directed towards each eye must be below 6.88\,W/sr.

The headset must also satisfy the weak-stimulus retinal thermal hazard criterion as an 850\,nm IR source is used and the exposure times are greater than 10\,s.

The 850\,nm source has a wavelength hazard weighting of:
%
\begin{equation}
    R(\lambda) = 0.501
\end{equation}

Which we use comparing the weighted source radiance against the allowable limit by:
%
\begin{equation}
    L \approx \frac{I}{l \cdot b} \; R(\lambda) < \frac{6000}{\alpha} = \frac{6000}{0.011} = 545.5 \; \text{kW/m}^{2}\text{sr}
\end{equation}
%
where $l$, $b$ are dimensions of the LED and $\alpha = 0.011$\,rad.

For example, an LED with $l, b = 1$\,mm and radiant intensity $I = 0.25$\,W/sr gives a weighted source radiance of 125\,kW/m\textsuperscript{2}sr which lies below the long exposure limit of 545.5\,kW/m\textsuperscript{2}sr, indicating a safe and attainable safety requirement.

Given the project brief outlined in \Cref{sec:electronics-requirements}, the power subsystem must satisfy 4 requirements: (i)~provide main supply for the compute subsystem (ii)~distribute secondary rails for lower power peripherals (iii)~support rechargeable operation with safety considerations (iv)~remain practically sized for headset mounting.

An obvious choice for the main power source was a LiPo battery, which is known for its versatility and use in portable electronics~\cite{tarascon2001lithium}\todo{Verify citation: original had [LiPo doc] --- replaced with Tarascon \& Armand 2001 (Nature review on rechargeable lithium batteries)}. However, a 2-cell (2S) LiPo configuration is preferred over the single cell as its higher voltage provides more efficient conversion to the supply rails (\Cref{fig:power-distribution}). Additionally, a split rail architecture has been adopted as the headset contains components with different electrical load requirements. The compute subsystem requires a high current 5\,V rail and IMUs are better suited to a 3.3\,V rail.

\subsubsection{Power Budget and Battery Sizing}

The size of the battery is approximated by the headset's energy consumption per test then scaled to the number of tests per charge. At this stage, published figures are used for subsystem power demands instead of measured values in \Cref{tab:power-budget}. The Arducam OV9281 is the chosen camera module and the Raspberry Pi~5 is the compute platform, which are used for the power calculations.

\begin{table}[htbp]

    \caption{Estimated power budget by subsystem and operating state.}
    \label{tab:power-budget}
    \begin{tabular}{lcl}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Subsystem / operating state} & \textbf{Representative power} & \textbf{Operating times} \\

        IR illumination subsystem                        & 2.0\,W [estimated]  & Pre-Capture, Capture \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The test for each biomarker (\ab{plr}, smooth pursuit, vergence) was timed to be 30\,s pre-test IR illumination, 30\,s active capture with stimulus and 30\,s post-capture inference.

The energy consumption per test is therefore approximated as:
%
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{test}} = P_{\text{pre}} \, t_{\text{pre}} + P_{\text{cap}} \, t_{\text{cap}} + P_{\text{inf}} \, t_{\text{inf}}
\end{equation}
%
where
%
\begin{align}
    P_{\text{pre}} &\approx 6.0 + 2.0 = 8 \; \text{W} \notag \\
    P_{\text{cap}} &\approx 6.0 + 0.4 + 2.0 + 0.01 + 2.0 = 10.41 \; \text{W} \notag \\
    P_{\text{inf}} &\approx 9.8 \; \text{W} \notag
\end{align}

Therefore:
%
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{test}} \approx 8.0 \times \frac{30}{3600} + 10.41 \times \frac{30}{3600} + 9.8 \times \frac{30}{3600} \approx 0.24 \; \text{Wh}
\end{equation}

The design target set was to achieve 30 tests, which amounts to about 10 users tested across all 3 biomarkers. Therefore:
%
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{total}} \approx 30 \times 0.24 = 7.2 \; \text{Wh}
\end{equation}

Allowing for a practical margin of 25\% to account for losses, gives a design target of 10\,Wh. For a 7.4\,V 2S LiPo battery, this gives a capacity of:
%
\begin{equation}
    C \approx \frac{10}{7.4} \approx 1.35 \; \text{Ah}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Charging and Safety Considerations}

The principal risks associated with the battery are (i)~protected battery charging, (ii)~over-current and under-voltage protection and (iii)~controlled low-battery shutdown. To meet these, a designated charge management path is used for the 2S LiPo battery. The battery will be charged via USB-C and the device will stay turned off during charging.

The headset will also include battery-state monitoring via a fuel-gauge IC connected to the compute subsystem. This allows for battery state to be logged and a shut-down procedure to be initiated when a lower operating limit is reached.

\section{Ergonomics and Assembly}

The optical and electronic subsystems above define the measurement chain in principle, but only deliver useful data if the headset is stable enough to keep the eye in the camera's working volume across the $\sim$10-min PLR protocol, comfortable enough that users tolerate that interval, and robust enough for shared pitch-side use. \Cref{fig:housing} reports the realised housing geometry, mass, and centre of mass that anchor the design choices below.

\textbf{Retention and mass.} The forward-mounted optics place the assembly's CoM 22.4\,mm forward of the face plane (\Cref{fig:housing}), generating a pitching torque that, left unreacted, slides the headset down onto the cheeks and displaces the eye-box. A three-point harness, comprising two side straps and a top strap meeting at a padded occipital cradle, was selected to convert this forward torque into distributed upward load on the crown; a single rear adjuster spans the head-size range. A simple elastic band fails to react the pitch torque, and a rigid halo (as on the Apple Vision Pro) is over-engineered for sub-10-min wear and slows donning. The complete 510\,g assembly (145\,g ABS shell, $\sim$250\,g electronics, $\sim$75\,g optics, $\sim$40\,g strap and pad) is comparable to commercial VR headsets (Meta Quest~3 $\sim$515\,g; Apple Vision Pro $\sim$600\,g). Cervical joint torques scale strongly with mass ($\sim$100\% RMS increase per +750\,g of head-mounted load) and more weakly with anterior CoM displacement ($\sim$20\% per +4\,cm)~\cite{astrologo2024hmd, chihara2018workload}; the near-symmetric CAD layout, with lateral and vertical CoM offsets below 0.3\,mm, keeps the design on the mass-driven, not CoM-driven, branch of this curve.

\textbf{Face seal and ventilation.} The PLR protocol requires controlled ambient illumination (\Cref{sec:bg-oculomotor}), so the face pad must seal against stray light while distributing pressure on a curved facial geometry and tolerating cleaning between users. A PU-leather-skinned closed-cell foam pad, 8--10\,mm thick, was selected over open-cell foam (absorbs perspiration, harbours bacteria) and over medical-grade silicone (preferred for production but slow to iterate at prototype stage); the leather skin is wipeable, the foam does not absorb sweat, and a snap-fit attachment isolates cleaning from the calibrated optical bench. A fully sealed enclosure, however, traps warm humid air from breath and skin that condenses on cool optical surfaces, so baffled vents along the lower shell edge, set below the camera optical axis (\Cref{fig:housing}), admit airflow while keeping any residual stray light outside the camera's field of view. \todo{Specify cleaning agent, frequency, and pad replacement schedule.}

\textbf{Shell.} The shell must survive transport in kit bags (summer temperatures can exceed 40\,\textdegree C), drops, and cleaning, while permitting iterative geometry changes during prototyping. ABS (HDT $\sim$85\,\textdegree C, well-characterised mechanical properties under realistic FDM conditions~\cite{tymrak2014mechanical}) was selected over PLA (HDT $\sim$55\,\textdegree C, brittle on impact) and PETG (HDT $\sim$70\,\textdegree C, marginal). The shell parts along the horizontal plane visible in \Cref{fig:housing}, allowing the optical bench, electronics tray, and battery to be installed before the halves are joined; all optical components are recessed within the shell, so drops contact the shell rather than the optics. SLS-printed PA~12 is retained as the production-stage target for fatigue and abrasion resistance and ISO~10993-5/-10 certification for limited skin contact in industry-standard grades (e.g.\ EOS PA~2200)~\cite{eos2024pa2200, bazan2023pa12}, but is excluded at this stage as SLS is incompatible with bench-top iteration.

With the housing fixed, the hardware now delivers a stable, time-stamped binocular video and IMU stream from a known eye--camera geometry. \Cref{chapter:software} takes that stream and develops the signal-processing and inference pipelines that produce the screening output.
