\chapter{Financial Plan}
\label{chapter:ent-financial}

This chapter builds a seven-year (2026--2032) financial projection from the operating model of \Cref{chapter:ent-business} and uses it to assess the venture's attractiveness. The analysis answers two questions: how large is the cash trough the venture must bridge, and which features of the profile are structural strengths or binding weaknesses.

Two scalar assumptions are load-bearing. \section{Base-Case Projection}
\label{sec:ent-projection}

\subsection{Unit economics}
\label{sec:ent-unit-economics}

\Cref{tab:ent-fin-unit-econ} builds the per-customer economics from the hardware and subscription contributions of \Cref{chapter:ent-business}: if a single institutional customer does not pay back, no amount of scale repairs the business.

\input{figures/financial/tab_unit_economics.tex}

At Phase~3 steady state the two channels show distinct unit economics. Institutional customers compound \pounds126 per-headset hardware contribution and \pounds510/yr per-headset subscription contribution into a three-year LTV of \pounds4{,}524 against a CAC of \pounds437 (10.4$\times$ LTV:CAC, 3.4-month payback), comfortably above the 3$\times$ benchmark in the subscription-business literature~\cite{gupta2004valuing}. DTC purchases are one-off hardware sales: \pounds126 contribution against \pounds90 CAC, a 1.4$\times$ ratio that just clears acquisition cost. Subscription revenue funds the institutional engine; DTC volume diversifies revenue and spreads the regulatory cost base over a larger installed base, but does not itself fund the company.

Aggregated over the horizon, these unit economics produce the trajectory of \Cref{tab:ent-fin-pnl} and \Cref{fig:ent-fin-pnl-overview}. Early years are pre-revenue: Phase~1 is a research validation deployment, and commercial sales begin only at UKCA certification.

\textbf{Revenue mix and gross margin.} Once commercial selling begins, hardware revenue grows with new unit shipments whilst institutional subscription revenue compounds on the cumulative installed base, with the recurring share reaching 47\% by Y7 (\Cref{fig:ent-fin-pnl-overview}\,a). Gross margin (\Cref{fig:ent-fin-pnl-overview}\,b) starts at 54\% in the institutional-only Phase~2 commercial launch (Y4), then rises to 62\% by Y7 as the Phase~3 unit cost of \Cref{tab:unit-cost-stack} takes effect and the higher-margin subscription line grows. Operating expenses rotate from R\&D-led to commercial-led over the same window (\Cref{fig:ent-fin-pnl-overview}\,c), the structural signature of a medtech venture maturing from product development to commercial execution.

\textbf{EBITDA trajectory and tax.} The EBITDA line (\Cref{fig:ent-fin-pnl-overview}\,d) traces the textbook medtech \emph{valley of death}: a Y2--Y5 loss plateau and a sharp Y6--Y7 climb-out as the cumulative installed base passes critical mass and the cost base stabilises. EBITDA reaches +\pounds149\,k (3.7\% margin) in Y7, delivering break-even within the modelled horizon. No corporation tax is owed across the seven years: cumulative trading losses of approximately \pounds4.3 million through Y6 exceed Y7 pre-tax profit, and the SME R\&D credit generates a \pounds22--78 thousand cash refund each year during the loss-making period.

\textbf{Cash trajectory and capital requirement.} The unfunded cash trajectory (\Cref{fig:ent-fin-cash-overview}\,a, dashed) strips out the illustrative staged equity injections to give the operating-and-investing reality of the venture's capital need. It falls through the regulatory investment phase, bottoms at approximately \pounds3.75 million in Y6, and climbs back in Y7 as net cash from operations turns positive. This trough is the gross capital requirement the venture must bridge from inception to self-funding.

\textbf{Working capital and capital expenditure.} Two mechanics shape the translation from P\&L to cash. Institutional subscriptions are billed annually in advance, so each new subscribing customer creates a deferred-revenue inflow that more than offsets the inventory and receivables build associated with hardware sales; the net working-capital change is a positive contributor to operating cash in every year of the horizon (\Cref{fig:ent-fin-cash-overview}\,b), accumulating to approximately \pounds373 thousand by Y7. Capital expenditure is dominated by the aluminium and steel injection-moulding tools of \Cref{sec:ent-manufacturing-strategy}; at \pounds112 thousand over the horizon it is immaterial relative to operating cash burn.

\section{Structural Assessment}
\label{sec:ent-structural}

\subsection{Structural strengths}
\label{sec:ent-strengths}

Three features are structural strengths of the business model, acting on three independent financial axes: margin, cash timing, and absolute capital required.

\textbf{Recurring revenue compounds into operating leverage.} The institutional unit economics of \Cref{sec:ent-unit-economics} (11.2$\times$ LTV:CAC, 3.2-month payback) compound on the cumulative installed base into a 40\% Y7 incremental EBITDA margin (\Cref{tab:ent-fin-pnl}), the fingerprint of the hybrid engine. Without the recurring layer, hardware alone would not reach Phase~3 grassroots prices: subscription revenue funds the regulatory build and sustains per-unit contribution through the cost-down to the mass-market price ceiling of \Cref{sec:ent-pricing-strategy}.

\textbf{Annual-in-advance billing creates a working-capital tailwind.} Hardware businesses typically absorb working capital as they grow; this venture reverses the sign. Each new institutional customer pays a full year's subscription on contract signature, generating a deferred-revenue inflow that more than offsets the inventory and receivables build of hardware sales, so net working capital contributes positively to operating cash in every year of the horizon (\Cref{fig:ent-fin-cash-overview}\,b). The Y7 cash conversion ratio (NCFO $\div$ EBITDA) reaches 261\%, a benefit unavailable to a per-test or pure-hardware revenue model.

\textbf{The absolute capital need is low by sector standards.} Capital expenditure totals only \pounds112 thousand across the horizon, dominated by the injection-moulding tools of \Cref{sec:ent-manufacturing-strategy}; the partial-turnkey arrangement of \Cref{sec:ent-mfg-sourcing} keeps production off the balance sheet. The Class~IIa regulatory pathway avoids the pivotal-trial expenditure characteristic of higher-class devices, and the resulting \pounds3.75 million peak trough compares favourably to the USD~25 million average medtech VC round~\cite{deloitte2022medtech}. UK tax treatment reinforces the profile: cumulative trading losses through Y6 shelter Y7 taxable income and carry forward indefinitely, and the SME R\&D credit refunds \pounds22--78 thousand in cash during each loss-making year.


\subsection{Binding weaknesses}
\label{sec:ent-weaknesses}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/financial/fig_sensitivity_overview.pdf}
    \caption[Sensitivity overview, two-panel summary]{Panel (a): tornado of Y7 EBITDA impact per driver (adverse moves red, favourable teal). Panel (b): percentage shock from base case that drives Y7 EBITDA to zero, the margin of safety in each lever's own units; tighter margins are more binding constraints on execution.}
    \label{fig:ent-fin-sensitivity}
\end{figure}

Three levers bind the venture's execution:

\textbf{Subscription-engine fragility.} Adoption dominates the tornado by more than a factor of two over any other lever: the hybrid is volume-led and cannot be rescued by price or cost adjustments. Churn ranks fourth on magnitude but is asymmetrically damaging on margin of safety, because LTV scales with its inverse. The two sharpen each other during Phase~2: each thin-margin hardware unit is an acquisition bet on subscription attach and retention, and if either fails the bridge does not form and Phase~2 hardware sales convert from lead generation to pure loss.

\textbf{Regulatory timing.} The regulatory-delay bar is a one-sided discrete shock: a one-year slip in UKCA certification produces an EBITDA hit comparable to the largest magnitude shocks, extending the pre-revenue phase by a year of cost base against no revenue.

\textbf{Phase~3 cost-down dependency.} The base case assumes the RK3588S migration of \Cref{sec:ent-sc-arch} lands on schedule, driving the Y5 unit-cost step from \pounds285 to \pounds174 (\Cref{tab:unit-cost-stack}). If it slips, hardware contribution collapses from \pounds126 to approximately \pounds15 per unit, erasing roughly \pounds1.4 million of cumulative Y5--Y7 contribution and driving Y7 EBITDA to \pounds(690)\,k at unchanged volumes. More fundamentally, the Phase~2 cost floor of \pounds252--318 sits at or above the \pounds250--350 grassroots price ceiling of \Cref{sec:ent-pricing-strategy}, foreclosing the Phase~3 mass-market route. Unlike regulatory timing, this is an engineering deliverable within the venture's direct control.

These individual bounds are necessary but not sufficient: a joint stress moving every driver adversely at once widens the peak unfunded trough to approximately \pounds6.7 million and drives Y7 EBITDA to \pounds(1.4) million, bounding the correlated risk that independent lever tracking cannot capture~\citep{saltelli2004sensitivity}.
