\section{System Overview}

The headset is designed such that on a Raspberry Pi~5 CPU, we must run a stimulus display, two camera streams at 120\,fps, an IMU polling loop, Bluetooth communication, and edge CNN inference. The software described in this chapter manages these concurrent workloads and implements the full pipeline from raw sensor data to a trial result, covering model training, real-time inference, signal quality filtering, metric extraction, and secure data handling. This allows us to understand the \ab{plr} and smooth pursuit biomarkers given the hardware designed in \Cref{chapter:headset} and trial structure in \Cref{chapter:introduction}.

For each of these biomarkers, we fine-tuned separate machine learning models. For smooth pursuit, we developed a CNN which determines the gaze angle given an eye image, enabling assessment of whether the user can follow a marker on the screen. For the \ab{plr} biomarker, a different CNN produces a binary mask indicating whether each pixel belongs to the pupil, from which pupil width is measured to assess dilation responses to light.

The software was designed to ensure a robust trial yielding accurate, repeatable, and reliable results whilst preserving data security in compliance with GDPR~\cite{ico_gdpr_minimisation} and HIPAA~\cite{hhs_hipaa_privacy}. We developed simple, validated methodologies that can be reused as hardware iterates and more headset data are collected. These methods prioritise fast iteration whilst preserving accuracy, keeping the product cost low and removing friction from development.

To ensure that the user can easily operate this system, a phone app controls the headset and displays results. A simple interface lowers the risk of users declining a test that could be instrumental for their safety, increasing user safety through reduced operating friction.

\section{On-Device Deployment}

\subsection{LiteRT Runtime}
\label{sec:litert}

As the models run entirely on the Pi~5 and the phone serves only as a user interface, runtime selection critically shapes the system's latency, memory footprint, energy consumption, and operational reliability. The stimulus display, IMU polling loop, Bluetooth communication, and cameras run concurrently, making efficient inference essential.

Three inference backends were evaluated for deploying MobileNetV2 on the Pi~5's Broadcom BCM2712 SoC: PyTorch, ONNX Runtime, and LiteRT (formerly TensorFlow Lite).

PyTorch was not selected as its primary function is as a training and research framework rather than a lightweight inference engine. Although real-time inference on the Pi~5 is feasible, the full framework is heavier than necessary for pure forward inference. ONNX Runtime was engineered as an inference-focused runtime with a documented Raspberry Pi deployment path~\cite{onnxruntime2021}.

Comparative studies of energy consumption show that ONNX Runtime and LiteRT are more energy-efficient than PyTorch for MobileNetV2 CPU workloads, benefiting from XNNPACK-optimised kernels~\cite{malenza2025energy}. This is also important for \textbf{environmental reasons}, making sure that our carbon footprint is under check. However, these benchmarks were conducted on different hardware, requiring task-specific benchmarking on the Pi~5 for definitive conclusions which is a \textbf{limitation} and something we will do on our prototype to ensure of. Benchmarking on Pi ARM hardware confirms that runtime choice affects inference performance, with LiteRT performing strongly, particularly when the model is further quantised to INT8~\cite{baller2021deepedgebench}.

For FP32 inference, the literature does not show a clear advantage between ONNX Runtime and LiteRT, but both outperform PyTorch on MobileNetV2 CPU workloads. The strongest evidence favours LiteRT in quantised settings on the Pi~4. Although exact Pi~5 benchmarking was not available, we benchmarked the three runtimes on an Apple Silicon ARM CPU using four threads and FP32 precision to compare relative performance. The Pi also uses the ARM instruction set, so relative performance rankings should transfer even though absolute latencies will differ. Over 200 inference passes with a 30-iteration warmup, LiteRT achieved the lowest mean latency at 2.68\,ms, followed by ONNX Runtime at $1.21\times$ slower, and PyTorch at $8.34\times$ slower. LiteRT also exhibited the most consistent inference times with a standard deviation of 0.12\,ms. The memory difference also isn't a concern as its negligible compared to  (\Cref{tab:runtime-benchmark}).

\subsection{INT8 Quantisation as Future Improvement}
\label{sec:int8}

The present software uses FP32 deployment to avoid introducing quantisation errors in the gaze estimation pipeline. A future optimisation is INT8 quantisation, which would reduce model size by approximately $4\times$ and increase CPU inference efficiency. However, in MobileNet architectures, post-training quantisation (PTQ) can cause large accuracy degradation~\cite{yun2021mobilenets}, whereas quantisation-aware training (QAT) preserves accuracy by exposing the model to quantisation effects during training~\cite{google_qat}. Due to the additional effort required, FP32 was used for simplicity and to achieve initial validation, with INT8 QAT planned as a future optimisation. This reflects the engineering philosophy of establishing a simple and reliable baseline before introducing further optimisations.


\section{Gaze Estimation}
\label{sec:gaze-estimation}

\subsection{Smooth Pursuit Trial}

Smooth pursuit eye movement impairments following \ab{mtbi}, as detailed in \Cref{chapter:background}, manifest as two primary deficits:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Saccadic jumps:} the eyes fall behind the tracked object and then jump forward to catch up.
    \item \textbf{Latency:} a delay between when the object starts moving and when the eyes begin to follow.
\end{enumerate}

During the smooth pursuit trial, the user follows a marker moving sinusoidally across the display. The camera captures eye images, and the gaze estimation model produces predicted gaze angles that are compared against the ground truth target angle to derive a set of metrics indicating whether underlying pursuit issues are present.

\subsection{Smooth Pursuit Metrics}

The following metrics are extracted from the pursuit signal:

\textbf{Pursuit gain.} Calculated by taking the FFT of both signals (the target motion is sinusoidal) and analysing the amplitudes at the stimulus frequency. Gain is defined as $\text{Amplitude}_{\text{eye}} / \text{Amplitude}_{\text{target}}$, and should be close to unity, with lower values indicating poor performance~\cite{devillerssidani2023oculomotor}.

\textbf{Catch-up saccade activity.} The number of catch-up saccades in the pursuit signal. The algorithm calculates eye velocity from the gaze estimation signal and detects saccades where velocity exceeds a threshold, retaining only events occurring whilst the eye is behind the target and the jump reduces the tracking error~\cite{devillerssidani2023oculomotor}.

\textbf{RMS tracking error.} The root-mean-square difference between the measured eye angle and the expected target angle, robust to sign cancellation~\cite{kellar2018comparing}.

\textbf{Lag.} The temporal delay between the target trajectory and the eye response, calculated via cross-correlation between the gaze estimation signal and the target signal. The time offset maximising the correlation peak gives the pursuit lag, consistent with prior work treating lag as a key parameter~\cite{devillerssidani2023oculomotor}.

The module also outputs two quality-related quantities. \textbf{Motion validity} is derived from IMU-based gating logic and indicates whether excessive head movement compromised the trial. \textbf{Signal confidence} reflects the reliability of the eye-tracking signal, derived from analysis of blur, blinking, and glare (detailed in \Cref{sec:quality-checks}).

\subsection{Decision Logic}

The metrics are subject to decision logic that first determines whether the trial was valid. For the IMU signal, trials where acceleration exceeds 1.5\,g for more than 300\,ms at any point are rejected. For signal confidence, if more than 10\% of samples are rejected due to blur, blinking, or glare, the trial is classified as inconclusive. These thresholds are empirically chosen to balance realistic defects with accuracy, and will be refined as more data are collected on the headset.

Pursuit gain is used as the primary metric for detecting smooth pursuit dysfunction, as it is the most clinically established measure~\cite{leigh2015neurology}. The remaining metrics (lag, saccade count, RMS tracking error) are reported as metadata to provide clinicians with additional context for informed decision-making. A pursuit gain below 0.85 at a stimulus frequency of 0.16\,Hz is used as the threshold for flagging potential impairment, based on the observation that healthy individuals typically achieve gains of 0.9--1.0 at frequencies $\leq 0.4$\,Hz~\cite{meyer2020smoothpursuit}, placing the threshold below the normal range whilst accounting for measurement uncertainty in the gaze hardware.

\subsection{Data Collection Pipeline}
\label{sec:data-pipeline}

For both \textbf{training and testing}, the data streams recorded are: display angle, timestamps, IMU motion stream, quality indicators of the eye signal, and a signal derived from the eye. This derived signal is the raw eye image when collecting for training, and the predicted gaze angle when testing.

The \textbf{core difference} is that in training mode the goal is to collect eye images with ground truth angles, whilst in testing the goal is to produce estimated gaze angles for comparison against the ground truth display angle to assess smooth pursuit performance.

The \textbf{data retention} schemes differ between modes. In training, the system retains raw eye imagery together with timeseries, event logs, and session metadata. Data is written locally in buffered chunks of approximately 5~seconds, uploaded via WiFi, and then deleted to manage storage. This chunk duration represents an engineering trade-off: longer buffers risk data loss during upload delays, whilst more frequent transfers incur excessive overhead.

In testing, the system stores the aligned timeseries, extracts pursuit metrics, session metadata, and the final trial result, discarding raw eye video after calculating the image confidence score and estimated gaze angle. Raw imagery is deleted because 120\,fps capture generates approximately 2\,GB per 30-second session, and the raw frames are not required after metric extraction. Deletion also ensures compliance with data minimisation principles under GDPR~\cite{ico_gdpr_minimisation} and HIPAA~\cite{hhs_hipaa_privacy}. The compact 5\,kB result is transmitted to the user's phone via Bluetooth, with session summaries cached locally until successful transfer to handle sparse connectivity. This reflects an edge-computing architecture where sensitive data is processed locally with deferred bulk transfer to reduce real-time bandwidth requirements~\cite{rancea2024edge}.

Both training and testing require \textbf{synchronisation} of the ground truth signal and the gaze estimation or image signal. The monotonic timestamps recorded by the system are not exactly the times of the events: $T_{\text{marker}} = t_{\text{monotonic}} + \Delta t_{\text{screen}}$ and $T_{\text{gaze}} = t_{\text{monotonic}} - \Delta t_{\text{image-to-CPU}}$. In this prototype, synchronisation uses monotonic clock timestamps directly rather than estimating these delays. This simplification avoids introducing additional calibration complexity at an early development stage. A future improvement would estimate these delays through explicit hardware calibration, enabling alignment on event times rather than software timestamps.

\subsection{Signal Quality Checks}
\label{sec:quality-checks}

During testing, raw data from the sensors and gaze estimation model must be cleaned for accurate inference. Regions of low confidence arise from blur, glare, and blinking. Removing these is essential as they can corrupt metrics and produce misleading results. Filtering is also necessary to remove noise artefacts that imitate poor pursuit performance. This is most critical for saccade count, as catch-up saccade detection is the most noise-sensitive downstream metric~\cite{birawo2022review, leube2017sampling, cheng2026eye}. Unlike gain or RMS tracking error, which are computed over longer temporal windows, saccadic activity depends on brief high-velocity events and is therefore far more vulnerable to noise peaks. Simultaneously, over-filtering must be avoided to preserve genuine pursuit artefacts.

The gaze estimation model outputs only angles without confidence scores. This was a deliberate design decision, as uncertainty-aware models require more accurate training labels and careful calibration~\cite{kendall2017uncertainties, kuleshov2018accurate}. Zheng et al.\ show that uncertainty-aware gaze estimation models can produce inconsistent and unreliable uncertainty estimates, with accuracy improvements being incremental compared to existing external quality check algorithms~\cite{zheng2025improving}.

\textbf{Blur and blink detection.} The variance of the Laplacian of the image is used with a cutoff of 1.6, rejecting all frames with variance $\leq 1.6$. Blinks produce images similar to blur and are detected using the same method.

\textbf{Glare detection.} The saturated pixel ratio is used with a threshold of 1\%, allowing some glare whilst preventing metric corruption. This provides a balance of accuracy and practicality.

All cutoffs are determined empirically and can be updated as more data are collected from the real system using the same methodology.

\subsection{Signal Filtering}
\label{sec:filtering}

The goal of filtering is to preserve genuine poor pursuit behaviour whilst removing artificial noise artefacts, enabling accurate determination of pursuit issues. To validate the filtering methodology, synthetic pursuit trajectories were generated ranging from ideal (no saccades) to severely impaired (high saccade count). The true saccade count was compared against the count derived from the filtered signal, using $\sigma = 0.6$ noise addition as the test parameter. Saccade count was chosen to guide filter performance as it is the most noise-sensitive downstream metric. This is supported by the eye movement detection literature, where saccades are detected using velocity-threshold algorithms~\cite{birawo2022review}, making them prone to noise after differentiation. The filter was thus tuned against the most fragile part of the metric pipeline, ensuring adequacy for the slower, lower-frequency measures such as gain and RMS tracking error.

The filter parameters can be refined using the same methodology as more data become available. Additionally, more data on glare, blurring, and head movement will be collected to develop more accurate threshold cutoffs using the algorithms described above.

\subsection{Training Pipeline}
\label{sec:gaze-training}

\subsubsection{Model Selection}

The CNN used for gaze estimation is MobileNetV2~\cite{sandler2018mobilenetv2}, selected because it is explicitly designed for mobile and embedded vision workloads where inference must be fast and memory-efficient. Its architectural features---linear bottlenecks, inverted residual blocks, and depthwise separable convolutions---reduce parameter count and multiply-accumulate operations compared with heavier alternatives whilst preserving representational capacity. A plausible alternative would have been ResNet-18~\cite{he2016deep}, which is widely used for computer vision tasks but is computationally heavier. Since the additional capacity was not required, MobileNetV2 was preferred to optimise for faster and more efficient edge deployment.

\subsubsection{Regression Head Architecture}

The MobileNetV2 backbone was adapted for gaze-angle regression by replacing the ImageNet classifier with a custom regression head~\cite{sandler2018mobilenetv2}. The 1280-dimensional feature vector produced by MobileNetV2 is passed through: Dropout ($p = 0.2$), a linear reduction from 1280 to 512 dimensions, ReLU activation, a second Dropout layer ($p = 0.2$), and a final linear projection to a single gaze-angle output.

ReLU introduces non-linearity enabling the head to learn complex mappings, whilst the Dropout layers prevent overfitting to subject-specific appearances~\cite{srivastava2014dropout}, improving generalisation to new users. The 512-dimensional hidden layer represents a trade-off: a larger layer increases overfitting risk on small datasets, whilst a narrower layer may underfit if the mapping is too constrained.

The backbone was fine-tuned from the start of training rather than frozen, as the domain gap between ImageNet photographs and near-infrared eye images is large enough that the pretrained features must adapt beyond the final layers alone. This is consistent with the transfer learning literature, which shows that feature transferability decreases as target tasks and input distributions diverge from the pretraining domain~\cite{yosinski2014transferable}.

\subsubsection{Loss Function and Optimiser}

MSE loss was selected for 1D angular regression, which is optimal for continuous outputs with Gaussian noise. Both Zhang et al.~\cite{zhang2015appearance} and Krafka et al.~\cite{krafka2016eye} use MSE loss for gaze regression, establishing it as a conventional choice for this task.

Adam~\cite{kingma2015adam} with a learning rate of 0.001 was selected for early testing, with SGD with momentum planned for once the hardware design has stabilised. Chugh~\cite{chugh2020eye} found that SGD with momentum outperformed Adam in eye tracking experiments, achieving lower final validation loss despite slower initial convergence. This is consistent with the broader optimisation literature, where Adam tends to converge to sharp minima that generalise poorly, whereas SGD's noisier gradient estimates favour flatter minima with better generalisation~\cite{keskar2017large, wilson2017marginal}.

Adam is used initially because stable and rapid convergence is the immediate need with small initial datasets, where the model and preprocessing pipeline are still being iterated. A linear warmup schedule increases the learning rate from near-zero to the target over the first 5 epochs. This is essential because the regression head is randomly initialised whilst the backbone carries pretrained weights; without warmup, large initial gradients from the head would propagate backward and destroy pretrained features before alignment with the new task. After warmup, a ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler halves the learning rate if validation loss stalls for 5 consecutive epochs.

\subsubsection{MPIIGaze Benchmark Validation}

To validate these architectural choices independently of the headset hardware, the model was evaluated on the MPIIGaze benchmark~\cite{zhang2015appearance}, a public dataset containing 213{,}659 normalised eye patches from 15 subjects with calibrated 3D gaze direction labels. A single leave-one-person-out fold was used, keeping all images from one subject for testing and the remaining 14 for training. This protocol ensures that validation accuracy reflects cross-subject generalisation; note that the results are from a single fold and are therefore not directly comparable to published 15-fold means.

\subsection{Summary}

We have developed a complete software pipeline for training and testing the smooth pursuit trial, with robust preprocessing and training methodologies that can be reused as more headset data are collected and the hardware design iterates. The key limitation is that no actual headset training data were available; the pipeline was validated on MPIIGaze only. Next steps include collecting headset data and optimising filter, model, and threshold parameters accordingly.


\section{Pupil Width Estimation}
\label{sec:pupil-width}

This section describes the pipeline that converts raw eye images from the headset camera into \ab{plr} biomarker measurements. The pipeline must satisfy the following requirements, derived from the literature review  (\Cref{chapter:background}) and the headset hardware (\Cref{chapter:headset}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Precision.} Resolve the pupil diameter changes associated with \ab{plr} abnormalities in \ab{mtbi}, which manifest as differences of 15--20\% in constriction amplitude and 15--20\,ms in latency between concussed and healthy populations;~\cite{master2020pupillary, ciuffreda2017plr}).

    \item \textbf{Robustness.} Handle blinks (100--400\,ms of complete data loss) and other transient artefacts gracefully, detecting and discarding corrupted frames rather than propagating erroneous measurements.

    \item \textbf{Ease of use.} Operate without specialist training or equipment, producing measurements that are comparable across subjects.

    \item \textbf{Inference.} Complete processing within the 8.3\,ms per-frame budget imposed by the headset's 120\,fps capture rate on the Raspberry Pi~5.

\end{itemize}


\subsection{Pipeline Design}
\label{sec:pipeline}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{pipeline_figure.pdf}
    \caption{Pupil width estimation pipeline overview. (a)~Preprocessing: raw eye images undergo contrast enhancement. (b)~Direct regression: a CNN predicts pupil diameter as a single scalar. (c)~Semantic segmentation: a CNN produces per-pixel class labels from which iris and pupil boundaries are extracted via ellipse fitting, yielding multiple outputs including confidence. (d)~Biomarker extraction: per-frame measurements are accumulated into a time series and fitted with a parametric PLR model to extract clinically relevant biomarkers.}
    \label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

Two approaches dominate the recent literature for learning-based pupil measurement. In a \emph{regression} approach, a model predicts ellipse parameters directly from the raw image~\cite{kothari2021ellseg, jia2024condseg}. In a \emph{segmentation} approach, the model classifies each pixel into anatomical regions (pupil, iris, sclera, background) and an ellipse is fitted to the segmented boundary~\cite{chaudhary2019ritnet}.

We adopt segmentation. On \textbf{accuracy}, recent comparative work shows complementary strengths: regression achieves tighter ellipse centre localisation (1.12 vs 1.47\,px on OpenEDS), while segmentation produces higher-quality boundaries (97.4\% vs 94.6\% iris \ab{iou})~\cite{jia2024condseg, kothari2021ellseg}. Since our downstream task (tracking diameter changes over time) depends on boundary precision rather than absolute centre accuracy, segmentation is slightly better suited.

On \textbf{robustness}, segmentation produces a per-pixel probability map from which we derive per-frame confidence scores for automated quality gating: frames where the model is uncertain are downweighted or discarded before biomarker extraction, preventing corrupted measurements from propagating downstream. Regression provides no equivalent signal. On \textbf{interpretability}, the segmentation mask is directly inspectable: when the pipeline reports an anomalous \ab{plr} measurement, a user or developer can examine the mask to determine whether the anomaly reflects a genuine physiological response or a processing artefact.

The trade-off is \textbf{annotation cost} when adapting to new hardware. Pixel-level segmentation masks require minutes per image to produce, whereas regression labels (e.g.\ clicking two points on the pupil boundary) take seconds, making regression substantially cheaper to fine-tune on a new device. We offset this by training on existing datasets (OpenEDS; 12\,759 masks~\cite{garbin2019openeds}), applying domain-specific augmentation (\Cref{sec:domain-transfer}), and validating end-to-end using cheaply acquired regression-style diameter labels from our headset.

For a screening tool whose output may prompt referral to a medical professional, the robustness and interpretability advantages take priority over annotation cost: silent failures risk missed referrals, and the ability to audit predictions is essential for trust in the system's output. \Cref{tab:design-matrix} summarises the comparison.

\subsection{Ablation}
\label{sec:ablation}

Within the segmentation pipeline, three factors must be configured: model architecture, loss function, and preprocessing. We ablate each on the OpenEDS benchmark to verify our choices. As shown in \Cref{tab:baseline-results}, performance is saturated across architecture and loss function choices; preprocessing is the only ablated factor that meaningfully affects accuracy.

All models are trained on OpenEDS~\cite{garbin2019openeds} (12\,759 annotated images, 152 participants) with the Adam optimiser (learning rate $10^{-3}$), ReduceLROnPlateau scheduling, for 50 epochs on an NVIDIA RTX A4000 (16\,GB). Due to compute constraints, we run three seeds for one configuration per architecture (RITnet + Dice, U-Net + CE) to characterise run-to-run variance, and single seeds for the remaining configurations.

\paragraph{Architecture.} Deployment on the headset's Raspberry Pi~5 imposes inference compute constraints (the 8.3\,ms per-frame budget at 120\,fps has not yet been verified on-device). We compare RITnet~\cite{chaudhary2019ritnet}, a lightweight DenseNet--U-Net hybrid (250K parameters, {<}1\,MB), against a standard U-Net (7.8M parameters, 29.6\,MB). As \Cref{tab:baseline-results} shows, both architectures achieve comparable pupil axis error (0.09 vs 0.13 $\pm$ 0.02\,px), but U-Net is 46\% slower and 30$\times$ larger.

\paragraph{Loss function.} Not all pixels matter equally for diameter estimation: accuracy depends on the pupil--iris boundary. We compare cross-entropy, generalised Dice (which addresses class imbalance by weighting each class inversely by area), CE + Dice, and a compound loss adding a boundary-aware term that upweights pixels near class edges~\cite{chaudhary2019ritnet}. The spread in axis error across all four is 0.04\,px (\Cref{tab:baseline-results}), comparable to the $\pm$0.03\,px seed-to-seed variance observed for Dice. Since the differences are within noise, we adopt the compound loss following the original RITnet paper: the boundary-aware term is theoretically motivated for our boundary-sensitive task, even though its empirical effect is small at this dataset scale.

\paragraph{Preprocessing.} Raw eye images exhibit low contrast between iris and pupil. We apply gamma correction ($\gamma = 0.8$, a nonlinear intensity mapping that brightens dark regions) and CLAHE (contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalisation; clip limit 1.5, tile grid $8 \times 8$), which enhances local contrast without amplifying noise globally. Removing both approximately triples axis error (0.10 to 0.32\,px), making preprocessing the only factor with a meaningful effect.

\subsection{PLR Biomarker Extraction}
\label{sec:plr-extraction}

This subsection describes how per-frame segmentation masks are converted into clinically relevant \ab{plr} biomarkers. Each step is presented alongside the evidence that it works.

\paragraph{Diameter extraction.} Given a segmentation mask, we fit an ellipse to the largest pupil contour via least squares, yielding centre coordinates, major and minor axis diameters, and rotation angle. We report mean diameter (average of major and minor axes) for robustness to foreshortening from small head rotations within the headset.

The standard approach applies \texttt{argmax} to the segmentation logits before fitting, discarding the model's uncertainty. We improve on this with a probability-weighted fit: each pixel's contribution is weighted by its softmax probability of belonging to the pupil class, so uncertain boundary pixels have less influence than confident interior pixels. \todo{Report axis error improvement: hard vs soft fitting on OpenEDS test set.}

We normalise pupil diameter by the simultaneously measured iris diameter, producing a dimensionless pupil--iris ratio. Adult iris diameter varies by only ${\sim}3.4\%$ across individuals (11.8\,mm $\pm$ 0.4\,mm~\cite{boyle2021hvid}), so this ratio reflects true pupil size changes rather than geometric scaling, removing the need for per-subject calibration.

\paragraph{Signal cleaning.} The raw diameter time series contains frame-to-frame jitter, data gaps from blinks, and low-frequency pupil oscillations (hippus, 0.5--2\,Hz). Three steps clean the signal before biomarker extraction. \textbf{Blink detection:} frames where the pupil area drops below 50\% of the pre-stimulus baseline or where the segmentation confidence falls below a threshold are identified; short blinks are interpolated, while blinks during the constriction phase invalidate the trial. \textbf{Outlier rejection:} frames where the frame-to-frame diameter change exceeds 8\,mm/s (approximately mean + 2\,SD of healthy peak constriction velocity~\cite{bremner2012pupillometric}) are treated as segmentation failures and interpolated. \textbf{Savitzky--Golay filtering:} a local polynomial is fitted to each window for temporal smoothing~\cite{savitzky1964smoothing}. Unlike a Gaussian kernel, this preserves the sharp constriction onset that is critical for accurate peak velocity estimation.

\paragraph{Parametric curve fitting.} Rather than extracting biomarkers from local derivatives of the smoothed signal (which amplify noise), we fit a parametric model to the entire post-stimulus waveform~\cite{fan2011modeling}:
%
\begin{equation}
    d(t) = d_0 - A \left(1 - e^{-(t - t_{\mathrm{onset}})/\tau_c}\right) e^{-(t - t_{\mathrm{onset}})/\tau_d}
    \label{eq:plr-model}
\end{equation}
%
where $d_0$ is the baseline diameter, $A$ is the constriction amplitude, $t_{\mathrm{onset}}$ is the latency, $\tau_c$ is the constriction time constant, and $\tau_d$ is the dilation recovery time constant. The model is fitted via nonlinear least squares with physiologically informed bounds on each parameter. This acts as a physics-informed regulariser: every data point contributes to every parameter estimate, and the model rejects solutions that would correspond to impossible pupil dynamics. The fitted parameters directly yield the clinically relevant biomarkers identified in \Cref{chapter:background}: constriction latency ($t_{\mathrm{onset}}$), constriction amplitude ($A/d_0 \times 100\%$), peak constriction velocity ($\max |d'(t)|$, derivable analytically), and recovery time constant ($\tau_d$). \Cref{fig:plr-fitted} shows an example fit.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{plr_fitted_curve}
    \caption{Parametric curve fitting applied to a synthetic \ab{plr} waveform ($\sigma = 0.2$\,mm noise). The fitted model (red) yields constriction latency, amplitude, peak velocity, and recovery time constant. $R^2 = 0.965$.}
    \label{fig:plr-fitted}
\end{figure}

We validate robustness by generating 100 synthetic \ab{plr} waveforms at each of six noise levels ($\sigma = 0.05$ to $0.5$\,mm) and measuring parameter recovery against known ground truth (\Cref{fig:robustness-noise}). The parametric fit maintains $R^2 > 0.9$ up to $\sigma \approx 0.3$\,mm. Latency is the most robust parameter (error {<}0.04\,s across all noise levels), while amplitude and recovery time degrade at higher noise. In a separate blink injection experiment (3 to 10 consecutive frames zeroed at random post-stimulus positions), $R^2$ remains above 0.99 across all blink durations, confirming that the blink detection and interpolation pipeline effectively neutralises data dropout.

\paragraph{Bilateral comparison.} The headset captures both eyes simultaneously, enabling bilateral \ab{plr} comparison. The relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD), defined as the asymmetry in \ab{plr} parameters between eyes, is one of the most clinically significant pupillary findings in neurological assessment: even when absolute parameters fall within normal range, inter-eye asymmetry can indicate unilateral injury. We compute parameters independently for each eye and report the inter-eye difference for each biomarker.


\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The current pipeline has four principal limitations that define the scope for future work. First, the segmentation model is trained on OpenEDS; despite domain-specific augmentation, the gap to headset imagery may ultimately require fine-tuning on headset-specific annotated data to achieve clinical-grade accuracy. Second, the \ab{plr} extraction pipeline has been validated on synthetic waveforms with known ground truth; clinical validation with concussed and healthy participants is required to confirm that the parametric model captures the full range of pathological \ab{plr} responses observed in \ab{mtbi}. Third, the robustness experiments assume additive Gaussian noise, which may not fully characterise the headset's artefact profile (e.g.\ motion blur from head movement, specular reflections from the beamsplitter). Fourth, each frame is segmented independently; incorporating temporal context could improve frame-to-frame consistency but at the cost of increased model complexity and latency.


\section{Vergence Measurement}

Vergence measurement reuses the gaze estimation pipeline described in \Cref{sec:gaze-estimation} to assess a different oculomotor biomarker: the \ab{npc}.
