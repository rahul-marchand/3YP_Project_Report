\section{Revenue Model}
\label{sec:ent-revenue}

Of the seven revenue models identified by \citet{osterwalder2010business}, only \textit{asset sale} and \textit{subscription} fit. Advertising is incompatible with a clinical context where trust depends on the device serving the user's health; per-test usage fees penalise the marginal screen most likely to catch a missed concussion, contradicting the routine-screening objective of \Cref{chapter:introduction}; brokerage, licensing, and leasing assume a role the venture does not occupy.

Adding a subscription layer to the hardware sale is commercially attractive: it scales with the installed base, funds post-market surveillance and software updates, and recovers value over the customer lifetime rather than at the point of sale~\cite{teece2010business}. Applied to individual users, however, it fails on two counts. \textit{Ethically}, charging for continued use of owned hardware is extractive in any consumer context, and particularly so for a health device: because concussion risk is sparse and unpredictable, the recurring fee creates a continuous incentive to cancel during the long intervals when no screen is needed, so the device is likely gated at the moment of injury, directly undermining the routine-screening mission of \Cref{chapter:introduction}. \textit{Commercially}, the same failure mode exposes the venture to asymmetric reputational and legal tail risk: a publicly-attributed missed concussion where the barrier was commercial rather than clinical is a venture-threatening outcome an asset-sale model does not generate. For institutions, by contrast, the subscription charges for data administration rather than screening access, a genuine complement a standalone device cannot deliver: multi-athlete roster management, longitudinal baselines across seasons, multi-operator accounts, and return-to-play tracking (\Cref{chapter:ent-competitive}). Clinical screening itself remains free with the hardware, so the ethical objection does not transfer, and institutional willingness-to-pay attaches to administration rather than to any individual's injury risk. The selected model is therefore a hybrid: hardware at a single price to all customers, with an optional institutional subscription.

The platform the subscription sustains is a complementary asset to the hardware \citep{teece1986complementary}: recurring institutional revenue funds the post-market surveillance, software updates, and evidence generation that credential the hardware for both segments (including individuals who never subscribe), and bridges the venture to Phase~3 economies of scale before thin per-unit hardware contribution (\Cref{sec:ent-unit-cost,sec:ent-pricing-strategy}) can carry the business on its own. The GTM-side validation of this ordering is in \Cref{chapter:ent-gtm}.

\subsection{MVP Definition}
\label{sec:ent-mvp-definition}
\label{sec:ent-roadmap}

Phase~1 of the roadmap developed in \Cref{chapter:ent-market} corresponds to the \ab{mvp} defined here; Phase~2 and~3 trajectories are developed in \Cref{sec:ent-manufacturing-strategy}. The MVP is a research validation device deployed under controlled conditions with academic and clinical partners, comprising the headset (\Cref{chapter:headset}), inference software (\Cref{chapter:software}), a basic operator application, and a documented calibration protocol. Adapting \citet{ries2011lean}'s lean startup methodology to a regulated device that cannot ship loosely, the MVP must resolve three leap-of-faith hypotheses before regulatory clearance: \textit{technical}, that the hardware produces eye-tracking data matching reference pupillometry on the biomarker signatures of \Cref{sec:bg-oculomotor}, with validation methodology in \Cref{chapter:software}; \textit{user}, that the form factor is tolerable to children, adolescents, and individuals immediately post-injury, measured by session completion rate and tolerability scoring; \textit{operator}, that a non-expert can position the device, run a session, and act on the result, measured by time-to-complete and protocol adherence. Failure of any one invalidates the business case.


\subsection{Supply Chain Architecture}
\label{sec:ent-sc-arch}
\label{sec:ent-sourcing-risks}

The first strategic question is which activities to perform in-house. The binding manufacturing constraint is the geometric alignment of beamsplitter, cameras, and eye position, which is both specialised to this device (Williamson) and the principal determinant of measurement quality (Prahalad). The second strategic question is which purchased components need special attention. Applying \citet{kraljic1983purchasing}'s classification of purchased items by profit impact and supply risk, three BOM items sit squarely in the strategic quadrant: the compute module, dual cameras, and custom optical elements are each technically differentiated, sourced from a narrow supplier base, and difficult to substitute without redesign. Passive electronics, fasteners, and packaging are leverage or non-critical commodities. Three specific risks in the strategic quadrant warrant attention.

First, the Raspberry Pi CM5 is single-sourced with a flat pricing policy and has risen approximately 70\% since its November 2024 launch, driven by LPDDR4 memory shortages~\cite{rpi-cm5-source}. The planned Phase~3 migration to an RK3588S-based module~\cite{orangepi-cm5-source} is both the primary unit-cost reduction initiative and the principal sourcing-risk mitigation; the carrier-board redesign and software porting are scoped as a Phase~2 engineering initiative so that Phase~3 can execute without delay. Second, the Arducam OV9281 camera modules suffer from integrator rather than sensor concentration: the underlying OmniVision sensor is in active production, but the pool of compatible global-shutter module integrators is effectively a single vendor~\cite{arducam-ov9281-source}. Lateral qualification of an equivalent module from Waveshare or a direct OEM is scoped during Phase~1. Third, the custom optical elements (beamsplitter, coated lenses) carry quality rather than availability risk: low-cost Asian suppliers exhibit unit-to-unit reflectance variation that propagates into measurement inconsistency. The procurement policy is to dual-source with strict incoming quality control on reflectance per lot.


\subsection{Manufacturing Strategy}
\label{sec:ent-mfg-sourcing}
\label{sec:ent-manufacturing-strategy}

The scope decisions of \Cref{sec:ent-sc-arch} remain stable across phases; their implementation evolves with volume and maturity, progressing through the three process-structure stages of \citet{hayes1979process}'s product-process matrix: job shop, batch, and connected line.

\textit{Phase~1} (job shop): enclosures produced in-house by FDM in PETG for rapid iteration; PCBs fabricated and assembled by a low-volume service~\cite{jlcpcb-source}; integration, alignment, and calibration by the founding team. Priority: iteration speed.

\textit{Phase~2} (batch): enclosures transition to outsourced SLS in PA12 nylon~\cite{3dprintuk-source}; alignment is performed against documented procedures with purpose-built jigs. Aluminium injection-moulding tooling (one-time GBP 8\,000--14\,000, per-part GBP 2--5) becomes the lower total-cost option at approximately 300--500 cumulative units~\cite{modernmoulds-source} and is committed once Phase~2 demand is validated. Priority: repeatability.

\textit{Phase~3} (connected line): steel injection moulding for enclosures (one-time GBP 15\,000--30\,000, per-part GBP 1--3). Assembly moves to a partial-turnkey contract manufacturer certified to ISO~13485 or operating under the venture's quality management system (\Cref{chapter:ent-regulatory}): the venture consigns the strategic-quadrant components of \Cref{sec:ent-sc-arch} (compute module, cameras, optics); the CM sources commodity items at an 8--15\% material markup and performs PCB fabrication, SMT assembly, enclosure manufacture, and build-order assembly; the venture retains final integration, calibration, and end-of-line test. Priority: throughput and unit cost.

\subsection{Inventory Strategy}
\label{sec:ent-inventory}

Institutional demand is functional, predictable and contracted per roster per season (\Cref{chapter:ent-market}), whilst individual grassroots demand at Phase~3 is less predictable. \citet{fisher1997supplychain} argues supply-chain design should match demand profile, and the venture's mixed profile requires a hybrid posture implemented at the item level as \citet{chopra2016supply}'s push--pull architecture. Strategic-quadrant items (\Cref{sec:ent-sc-arch}) are pushed: ordered ahead of demand against forecast and held in venture-controlled consignment, because a supply disruption on a strategic input would halt manufacturing. Commodity sourcing and assembly are pulled: executed by the contract manufacturer on actual build orders, because commodity suppliers are interchangeable and re-order lead times are short. The Kraljic strategic/commodity split therefore sits on the push--pull boundary, producing an assemble-to-order architecture.

The pushed strategic items (compute module, cameras, optics) carry 8--16 week lead times~\cite{rpi-cm5-source, arducam-ov9281-source, edmund-optics-source}; consignment is sized to the binding item (CM5 at 12--16 weeks), a strategic-item buffer of approximately 100 days. Against roughly 20 days of commodity and WIP inventory at the contract manufacturer, and with strategic items at two-thirds of BOM cost (\Cref{sec:ent-bom}), the blended target is 75 days inventory outstanding (DIO) on COGS.

The unit cost above is a constraint, not the price. Cost-plus is rejected in favour of value-based pricing with competitive anchoring~\cite{nagle2017pricing}: the buyer's perceived value is dominated by liability reduction, screening objectivity, and clinician time saved, not by the bill of materials. Willingness-to-pay (\Cref{chapter:ent-market}) and competitor positioning (\Cref{chapter:ent-competitive}) are adopted as inputs rather than re-derived here. Hardware is priced at a single tier across both segments, avoiding any form of price discrimination on a medical screening device. The price points below are working hypotheses to be validated in Phases~1--2.

The working hardware price is GBP 250--350, above the Phase~3 cost floor (GBP 138--209) but near or below the Phase~2 floor (GBP 252--318). It sits dramatically below clinical pupillometers and the EyeGuide Focus, and is comparable to the premium sports equipment (GPS vests, heart-rate monitors) that grassroots clubs already purchase (\Cref{chapter:ent-competitive}). Hardware sold at thin or negative margin during Phase~2 is therefore a deliberate acquisition strategy, sustainable only if institutional subscriptions attach and the resulting LTV exceeds CAC (\Cref{chapter:ent-gtm,chapter:ent-financial}). Distributor channel margins (typically 20--40\% in medtech, per \Cref{chapter:ent-gtm}) further compress the venture's wholesale realisation.

The institutional subscription is priced to fund the ongoing regulatory, clinical, and platform obligations that the product lifecycle requires whilst undercutting the software incumbents of \Cref{chapter:ent-competitive}; the working annual fee is adopted from the willingness-to-pay envelope of \Cref{chapter:ent-market}.\todo{Insert working subscription price once \Cref{chapter:ent-competitive,chapter:ent-market} are finalised.} The per-customer quantitative build (hardware contribution, LTV, CAC, LTV:CAC, payback) is developed in \Cref{sec:ent-unit-economics}.


\section{Operations}
\label{sec:ent-operations}

The per-customer contribution above must cover the venture's fixed cost base. Payroll, facilities, insurance, and regulatory obligations run at roughly constant rate regardless of revenue; that structure is the mechanical cause of the medtech valley of death analysed in \Cref{chapter:ent-financial}.

\label{sec:ent-ops-headcount}
\label{sec:ent-ops-org}
\label{sec:ent-ops-salaries}

The team maps onto \citet{mintzberg1979structuring}'s \textit{simple structure} in Phase~1, a four-founder nucleus spanning CEO/commercial, hardware/embedded, CV/software, and clinical/regulatory roles, and grows to 11--14 FTE by Phase~3 (\Cref{tab:ent-ops-headcount}). \subsection{Facilities, Capex, and Ongoing Costs}
\label{sec:ent-ops-facilities}
\label{sec:ent-ops-insurance}

The remainder of the fixed base scales with commercial exposure rather than with volume. Facilities step from free incubator space (Oxford University Innovation, Begbroke AgileLab) through Oxford coworking to dedicated premises at GBP 14--37\,k/yr once controlled optical alignment and regulatory document storage are required; cloud hosting rises from low thousands to GBP 20--50\,k/yr as longitudinal institutional data compounds on the installed base; manufacturing capex (the injection-moulding tooling of \Cref{sec:ent-manufacturing-strategy}) is immaterial at GBP 8--30\,k one-off. The structurally important line is product liability under strict liability (Consumer Protection Act 1987), which runs GBP 3--12\,k/yr at Phase~1 and GBP 20--45\,k/yr at Phase~3: NHS Supply Chain's GBP 5\,m cover minimum~\cite{nhs-supply-chain-insurance} is a non-discretionary floor. With the recurring QMS and post-market surveillance fees of \Cref{chapter:ent-regulatory}, Class~IIa compliance is a permanent feature of the operating base, concentrating the venture's commitment into recurring opex (the structural strength of \Cref{sec:ent-strengths}).


\subsection{Warranty and Returns}
\label{sec:ent-ops-warranty}

Hardware ships with a 12-month manufacturer's warranty, included in the hardware price of \Cref{sec:ent-pricing-strategy} and consistent with B2B medtech convention. Two disjoint accruals flow from it: a 2\% returns allowance booked as contra-revenue against dead-on-arrival units and 30-day institutional trial-period returns, and a 3\% warranty provision accrued at point of sale against 12-month repair and replacement costs. At the \pounds300 hardware ASP the latter is \pounds9 per unit, implying a 10\% failure rate at \pounds90 average repair cost, a conservative assumption for a first-generation optical device in sporting use.


\section{Synthesis}
\label{sec:ent-business-synthesis}

The chapter's central operational insight is that the subscription layer is not merely a pricing choice but an operations-timing one: Phases~1--2 consume engineering and regulatory capital precisely when per-unit hardware contribution is thinnest, and institutional revenue bridges the venture to the Phase~3 cost-down. The architecture is capital-light by construction, with partial-turnkey manufacturing off the balance sheet and a software platform that scales without proportional headcount, but concentrates execution risk on a single dependency: revenue viability, the manufacturing cost floor, and the pre-revenue burn all route through institutional attach and retention, stress-tested jointly in \Cref{chapter:ent-financial}. A strategic risk the sensitivity view does not surface is key-person concentration: a four-founder nucleus spans four critical functions, and the loss of any founder eliminates a capability until a specialist hire closes the gap, an exposure sharpened by the UK medtech regulatory-affairs scarcity of \Cref{sec:ent-ops-headcount}.
