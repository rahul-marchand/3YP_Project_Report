Preceding chapters established market opportunity and competitive context. This chapter builds the operating model that turns this into a business: revenue, supply chain, unit economics, and fixed cost base.


\section{Revenue Model}
\label{sec:ent-revenue}

Of the seven revenue models identified by \citet{osterwalder2010business}, only \textit{asset sale} and \textit{subscription} survive scrutiny. 

\textit{Advertising} is incompatible with a clinical context where trust is critical, and the addressable spend is in any case too small to sustain the venture. \textit{Per-test usage fees} penalise the marginal screen most likely to catch a missed concussion, contradicting the routine-screening objective of \Cref{chapter:introduction}. \textit{Brokerage} at its closest would mean operating an anonymised-data marketplace layered on hardware sales, a poor fit on three counts: it damages clinical trust just as advertising does; the addressable data market is smaller than the grassroots hardware market itself; and the revenue is parasitic on hardware sales, since data can only be collected from deployed devices. \textit{Leasing} converts the single hardware payment into an equal-NPV stream whilst transferring asset tracking, refurbishment, damage inspection, and retrieval onto a four-FTE startup (\Cref{sec:ent-ops-headcount}); the resulting operational overhead would require additional headcount and working capital with no revenue advantage over an outright sale.

\textit{Licensing} deserves separate scrutiny because it offers what no other rejected model does: recurring revenue without exposing the venture to the costs and risks of running a hardware supply chain. The natural form would be to license the inference stack to existing headset manufacturers (Meta Quest, Apple Vision Pro), turning pupillometric concussion screening into a software feature on hardware already in users' hands. Three obstacles foreclose this route. First, the problem the venture exists to solve is missed concussions in grassroots and youth sport (\Cref{chapter:introduction}); consumer VR headsets at GBP 400--3{,}500 reside with gamers and enterprise users, not with the clubs and schools that constitute the target population, so even a successful licence reaches the wrong market. Second, UKCA certification of the algorithm is bound to the specific camera placement, illumination, and timing characteristics validated during clearance (\Cref{chapter:ent-regulatory}); migrating to a third-party headset requires re-verification at the licensee's expense, eroding the licence's commercial value. Third, licensing the algorithm post-clearance surrenders the only moat the venture has built, since once the inference stack runs on commodity headsets the venture's hardware becomes redundant rather than complementary. Licensing is therefore better understood as an exit option than as a parallel revenue path.

Within asset sale and subscription, the optimal mix is fixed by the principle that revenue should attach to value delivery: a one-time payment at the point of asset transfer, a recurring fee against continuous service. The venture's two buyer segments consume different bundles. Individuals consume only the hardware itself, with no continuing service to attach a recurring fee to; layering a subscription on top would, given concussion's sparse and unpredictable timing, invite cancellation between events and gate access at the moment of injury, undermining the routine-screening mission and exposing the venture to reputational tail risk should a publicly-attributed missed concussion ever be traced to a commercial barrier. Institutions, by contrast, consume the hardware plus a continuous data-administration service that a standalone device cannot deliver: multi-athlete roster management, longitudinal baselines across seasons, multi-operator accounts, and return-to-play tracking (\Cref{chapter:ent-competitive}). The selected model is therefore a hybrid: hardware at a single price across both segments, with an institutional-only subscription priced against ongoing data administration. The data administration service is therefore a complementary asset \citep{teece1986complementary} to the hardware, with recurring institutional revenue funding the post-market surveillance, software updates, and evidence generation that credential the device for both segments and bridging the venture to economies of scale.

\subsection{MVP Definition}
\label{sec:ent-mvp-definition}
\label{sec:ent-roadmap}

Phase~1 of the roadmap developed in \Cref{chapter:ent-market} corresponds to the \ab{mvp} defined here; Phase~2 and~3 trajectories are developed in \Cref{sec:ent-manufacturing-strategy}. The MVP is a research validation device deployed under controlled conditions with academic and clinical partners, comprising the headset (\Cref{chapter:headset}), inference software (\Cref{chapter:software}), a basic operator application, and a documented calibration protocol. Adapting \citet{ries2011lean}'s lean startup methodology to a regulated device that cannot ship loosely, the MVP must resolve three leap-of-faith hypotheses before regulatory clearance: \textit{technical}, that the hardware produces eye-tracking data matching reference pupillometry on the biomarker signatures of \Cref{sec:bg-oculomotor}, with validation methodology in \Cref{chapter:software}; \textit{user}, that the form factor is tolerable to children, adolescents, and individuals immediately post-injury, measured by session completion rate and tolerability scoring; \textit{operator}, that a non-expert can position the device, run a session, and act on the result, measured by time-to-complete and protocol adherence. Failure of any one invalidates the business case.


\subsection{Supply Chain Architecture}
\label{sec:ent-sc-arch}
\label{sec:ent-sourcing-risks}

The first strategic question is which activities to perform in-house. Two frameworks converge: \citet{williamson1985institutions}'s transaction-cost economics keeps internal any activity of high \textit{asset specificity} (costly to redeploy if a supplier relationship breaks), and \citet{prahalad1990core}'s resource-based view retains any \textit{distinctive competency} (differentiating and costly to replicate). The binding manufacturing constraint is the geometric alignment of beamsplitter, cameras, and eye position, which is both specialised to this device (Williamson) and the principal determinant of measurement quality (Prahalad). The second strategic question is which purchased components need special attention. Applying \citet{kraljic1983purchasing}'s classification of purchased items by profit impact and supply risk, three BOM items sit squarely in the strategic quadrant: the compute module, dual cameras, and custom optical elements are each technically differentiated, sourced from a narrow supplier base, and difficult to substitute without redesign. Passive electronics, fasteners, and packaging are leverage or non-critical commodities. Three specific risks in the strategic quadrant warrant attention.

First, the Raspberry Pi CM5 is single-sourced with a flat pricing policy and has risen approximately 70\% since its November 2024 launch, driven by LPDDR4 memory shortages~\cite{rpi-cm5-source}. The planned Phase~3 migration to an RK3588S-based module~\cite{orangepi-cm5-source} is both the primary unit-cost reduction initiative and the principal sourcing-risk mitigation; the carrier-board redesign and software porting are scoped as a Phase~2 engineering initiative so that Phase~3 can execute without delay. Second, the Arducam OV9281 camera modules suffer from integrator rather than sensor concentration: the underlying OmniVision sensor is in active production, but the pool of compatible global-shutter module integrators is effectively a single vendor~\cite{arducam-ov9281-source}. Lateral qualification of an equivalent module from Waveshare or a direct OEM is scoped during Phase~1. Third, the custom optical elements (beamsplitter, coated lenses) carry quality rather than availability risk: low-cost Asian suppliers exhibit unit-to-unit reflectance variation that propagates into measurement inconsistency. The procurement policy is to dual-source with strict incoming quality control on reflectance per lot.


\subsection{Manufacturing Strategy}
\label{sec:ent-mfg-sourcing}
\label{sec:ent-manufacturing-strategy}

The scope decisions of \Cref{sec:ent-sc-arch} remain stable across phases; their implementation evolves with volume and maturity, progressing through the three process-structure stages of \citet{hayes1979process}'s product-process matrix: job shop, batch, and connected line.

\textit{Phase~1} (job shop): enclosures produced in-house by FDM in PETG for rapid iteration; PCBs fabricated and assembled by a low-volume service~\cite{jlcpcb-source}; integration, alignment, and calibration by the founding team. Priority: iteration speed.

\textit{Phase~2} (batch): enclosures transition to outsourced SLS in PA12 nylon~\cite{3dprintuk-source}; alignment is performed against documented procedures with purpose-built jigs. Aluminium injection-moulding tooling (one-time GBP 8\,000--14\,000, per-part GBP 2--5) becomes the lower total-cost option at approximately 300--500 cumulative units~\cite{modernmoulds-source} and is committed once Phase~2 demand is validated. Priority: repeatability.

\textit{Phase~3} (connected line): steel injection moulding for enclosures (one-time GBP 15\,000--30\,000, per-part GBP 1--3). Assembly moves to a partial-turnkey contract manufacturer certified to ISO~13485 or operating under the venture's quality management system (\Cref{chapter:ent-regulatory}): the venture consigns the strategic-quadrant components of \Cref{sec:ent-sc-arch} (compute module, cameras, optics); the CM sources commodity items at an 8--15\% material markup and performs PCB fabrication, SMT assembly, enclosure manufacture, and build-order assembly; the venture retains final integration, calibration, and end-of-line test. Priority: throughput and unit cost.


\subsection{Inventory Strategy}
\label{sec:ent-inventory}

Institutional demand is functional, predictable and contracted per roster per season (\Cref{chapter:ent-market}), whilst individual grassroots demand at Phase~3 is less predictable. \citet{fisher1997supplychain} argues supply-chain design should match demand profile, and the venture's mixed profile requires a hybrid posture implemented at the item level as \citet{chopra2016supply}'s push--pull architecture. Strategic-quadrant items (\Cref{sec:ent-sc-arch}) are pushed: ordered ahead of demand against forecast and held in venture-controlled consignment, because a supply disruption on a strategic input would halt manufacturing. Commodity sourcing and assembly are pulled: executed by the contract manufacturer on actual build orders, because commodity suppliers are interchangeable and re-order lead times are short. The Kraljic strategic/commodity split therefore sits on the push--pull boundary, producing an assemble-to-order architecture.

The pushed strategic items (compute module, cameras, optics) carry 8--16 week lead times~\cite{rpi-cm5-source, arducam-ov9281-source, edmund-optics-source}; consignment is sized to the binding item (CM5 at 12--16 weeks), a strategic-item buffer of approximately 100 days. Against roughly 20 days of commodity and WIP inventory at the contract manufacturer, and with strategic items at two-thirds of BOM cost (\Cref{sec:ent-bom}), the blended target is 75 days inventory outstanding (DIO) on COGS.

\section{Pricing}
\label{sec:ent-pricing-strategy}

The unit cost above sets a floor; willingness to pay (\Cref{chapter:ent-market}) sets a ceiling. Within that envelope the venture prices on a cost-plus basis rather than extracting up to willingness to pay~\citep{nagle2017pricing}. The choice is segment-driven: pure value-based extraction depends on buyers who can translate clinical, liability, and operational benefits into a single number~\citep{hinterhuber2008value}, which institutional sports-medicine procurers can do but the volunteer-run clubs and budget-constrained schools that constitute the bulk of the addressable market cannot. Cost-plus is more robust for these buyers on three counts: it is procurement-friendly, signalling transparent margins to non-specialist evaluators; it is reputationally defensible against any accusation of profiteering on a youth-safety device; and pricing near the cost floor positions the product alongside the premium sports equipment that grassroots clubs already accept (GPS vests, heart-rate monitors; \Cref{chapter:ent-competitive}) rather than at the clinical price points the segment would refuse. Hardware is priced at a single tier across both segments, avoiding price discrimination on a medical screening device.

Hardware is set at GBP 300, implying a 30--54\% gross margin at Phase~3 unit cost (\Cref{tab:unit-cost-stack}) and a thin-to-negative margin at Phase~2; the latter is a deliberate acquisition strategy, sustainable only if institutional subscriptions attach and the resulting LTV exceeds CAC (\Cref{chapter:ent-gtm,chapter:ent-financial}). The institutional subscription is set at GBP 600/yr per headset, sized to fund the post-market surveillance, software updates, and platform infrastructure of \Cref{sec:ent-revenue} at sustainable margin whilst undercutting the software incumbents of \Cref{chapter:ent-competitive}. Distributor channel margins (typically 20--40\% in medtech, per \Cref{chapter:ent-gtm}) further compress the venture's wholesale realisation. The per-customer quantitative build (hardware contribution, LTV, CAC, LTV:CAC, payback) is developed in \Cref{sec:ent-unit-economics}.

\subsection{Facilities, Capex, and Ongoing Costs}
\label{sec:ent-ops-facilities}
\label{sec:ent-ops-insurance}

The remainder of the fixed base scales with commercial exposure rather than with volume. Facilities step from free incubator space (Oxford University Innovation, Begbroke AgileLab) through Oxford coworking to dedicated premises at GBP 14--37\,k/yr once controlled optical alignment and regulatory document storage are required; cloud hosting rises from low thousands to GBP 20--50\,k/yr as longitudinal institutional data compounds on the installed base; manufacturing capex (the injection-moulding tooling of \Cref{sec:ent-manufacturing-strategy}) is immaterial at GBP 8--30\,k one-off. The structurally important line is product liability under strict liability (Consumer Protection Act 1987), which runs GBP 3--12\,k/yr at Phase~1 and GBP 20--45\,k/yr at Phase~3: NHS Supply Chain's GBP 5\,m cover minimum~\cite{nhs-supply-chain-insurance} is a non-discretionary floor. With the recurring QMS and post-market surveillance fees of \Cref{chapter:ent-regulatory}, Class~IIa compliance is a permanent feature of the operating base, concentrating the venture's commitment into recurring opex (the structural strength of \Cref{sec:ent-strengths}).


\subsection{Replacement, Warranty, and Returns}
\label{sec:ent-ops-warranty}

Hardware ships with a 12-month manufacturer's warranty, included in the hardware price of \Cref{sec:ent-pricing-strategy} and consistent with B2B medtech convention. Two disjoint accruals flow from it: a 2\% returns allowance booked as contra-revenue against dead-on-arrival units and 30-day institutional trial-period returns, and a 3\% warranty provision accrued at point of sale against 12-month repair and in-warranty replacement costs. At the \pounds300 hardware ASP the latter is \pounds9 per unit, implying a 10\% failure rate at \pounds90 average repair cost, a conservative assumption for a first-generation optical device in sporting use. Out-of-warranty replacement purchases triggered by end-of-life device wear are not modelled: the seven-year horizon of \Cref{chapter:ent-financial} is assumed to sit within a single product lifecycle, with full-lifecycle treatment deferred to \Cref{chapter:ent-risk}.


\section{Synthesis}
\label{sec:ent-business-synthesis}

The chapter's central operational insight is that the subscription layer is not merely a pricing choice but an operations-timing one: Phases~1--2 consume engineering and regulatory capital precisely when per-unit hardware contribution is thinnest, and institutional revenue bridges the venture to the Phase~3 cost-down. The architecture is capital-light by construction, with partial-turnkey manufacturing off the balance sheet and a software platform that scales without proportional headcount, but concentrates execution risk on a single dependency: revenue viability, the manufacturing cost floor, and the pre-revenue burn all route through institutional attach and retention, stress-tested in \Cref{chapter:ent-financial}. A strategic risk the sensitivity view does not surface is key-person concentration: a four-founder nucleus spans four critical functions, and the loss of any founder eliminates a capability until a specialist hire closes the gap, an exposure sharpened by the UK medtech regulatory-affairs scarcity of \Cref{sec:ent-ops-headcount}.
