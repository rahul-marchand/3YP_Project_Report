\section{Clinical Need}

\begin{figure}[b]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/intro_sankey.pdf}
    \caption{Estimated diagnostic pathway for mTBI in grassroots sport, showing where cases are currently missed. Proportions are approximate; see text for sources and discussion of evidence quality~\cite{mccrea2004unreported, echlin2010sideline, pryor2015athletic}.}
    \label{fig:intro_sankey}
\end{figure}

Mild traumatic brain injury (mTBI)\glsunset{mtbi}, commonly known as concussion, is the most prevalent form of neurological injury worldwide. An estimated 69 million people sustain traumatic brain injury annually~\cite{dewan2019estimating}, of which 70--90\% are classified as mild~\cite{cassidy2004incidence}. The majority occur in community and grassroots sport settings where no medical professional is present to assess the injured individual~\cite{pryor2015athletic}.

In these settings, parents, coaches, and referees act as de facto first responders but rely on visible signs and athlete self-reporting to decide whether medical evaluation is warranted. This approach is inadequate: approximately 50\% of sport-related concussions go unreported at the time of injury, with athletes failing to recognise the severity of their injury or being unwilling to remove themselves from competition~\cite{mccrea2004unreported, registermihalik2013knowledge}. Video and instrumented-helmet analyses confirm that impacts meeting concussion criteria are routinely missed sideline~\cite{echlin2010sideline, beckwith2013impacts}; \Cref{fig:intro_sankey} summarises the resulting diagnostic pathway.

The consequences of missed diagnosis are severe. Second impact syndrome, in which a second head injury is sustained before recovery from the first, causes catastrophic cerebral oedema, is frequently fatal, and is particularly devastating in the developing brain~\cite{sis_statpearls}. Post-concussive syndrome affects up to 15\% of adults following \ab{mtbi}, with 27\% of patients unable to return to their previous level of work at 12 months~\cite{ppcs_statpearls, mortaheb2021ppcs}. Early identification is therefore critical to enable appropriate management and prevent premature return to activity.


\section{Limitations of Existing Approaches}
\label{chapter:introduction:limitations}

\textbf{Accessible but subjective.} The Concussion Recognition Tool 6 (CRT6) was designed specifically for use by non-medical personnel such as parents, coaches, and referees~\cite{echemendia2023crt6}, but provides only observational screening: the observer must make subjective judgements about concerning signs, and the tool depends on athlete self-reporting in the very situations where athletes are motivated to minimise symptoms~\cite{meier2015underreporting}.

\noindent\textbf{Objective but inaccessible.} The \ab{scat6} provides comprehensive evaluation through symptom inventories, cognitive screening, neurological examination, and balance testing, but requires medical training to administer and interpret~\cite{echemendia2023scat6}. Commercial quantitative pupillometry devices offer objective measurement but cost thousands of pounds and are designed for medical facilities rather than field deployment. The most recent American Congress of Rehabilitation Medicine diagnostic criteria explicitly acknowledge that no validated stand-alone objective diagnostic test is available, even to trained clinicians~\cite{silverberg2023acrm}.


\section{Project Objectives}

Reducing missed concussions in grassroots sport therefore requires a screening tool that closes this gap, combining the field accessibility of the CRT6 with objective, quantitative measurement. Four requirements follow: the tool must be \textbf{accessible}, deployable in grassroots sport settings without specialised medical equipment or infrastructure; \textbf{usable by non-experts}, operable by parents, coaches, and referees without medical training or subjective interpretation; \textbf{objective and cooperation-independent}, providing quantitative measurement that does not depend on patient self-reporting or effort; and \textbf{rapid}, completable within minutes to enable use during or after sporting events.

This project develops a headset-based automated oculomotor screening system designed to \textbf{supplement, not replace,} existing non-expert tools such as the CRT6. \textbf{The system is a screening instrument, not a diagnostic tool}: it identifies individuals who may require further medical evaluation but does not provide a clinical diagnosis of \ab{mtbi}, and a negative result does not constitute medical clearance to return to play. \Cref{chapter:background} presents the rationale for oculomotor screening, and \Cref{chapter:headset} and \Cref{chapter:software} the headset and software design. \Crefrange{chapter:ent-market}{chapter:ent-risk} then examine the commercial pathway from prototype to deployable product.
